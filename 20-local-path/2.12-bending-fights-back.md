# Lesson 2.12: Why Bending the Model Sometimes Fights Back

Lesson 2.11 showed that a LoRA tilts the whole model toward your character. This lesson is about what happens when you tilt too hard. A base model has a strong sense of how the visual world normally looks, and training pushes against that. Push gently and it cooperates. Push too far and it resists, in ways that can look like random failure until you understand the cause. Think of it as bending a stiff material: a little flex holds, too much and it springs back or snaps.

## The tension at the heart of training

Training is a tug of war. On one side, your dataset says "make this specific character." On the other, the base model's deep training says "but this is how faces and bodies and scenes normally work." A good LoRA finds a truce. A LoRA pushed too hard tries to overpower the base model, and the base model fights to snap back to its normal behavior. That fight is what you see as a broken result.

> **The base model has opinions. Train hard enough to override them and it pushes back instead of obeying.**

## What "fighting back" looks like

- **Distorted or fried output.** Faces and anatomy break down, colors go strange, fine detail turns to mush. The model is being forced past where it bends cleanly.
- **Rigidity.** The character comes out locked to memorized poses and refuses new scenes, the overtraining failure from Lesson 2.9, seen from the model's side.
- **Instability.** Small prompt changes cause wild swings, because you are working in a strained region where the model is barely holding together.
- **Stubborn artifacts.** Recurring glitches you cannot prompt away, because they come from the strain itself, not your words.

> **[FILL LATER: fight-back gallery]** Your real examples of a model fighting back: the fried results, the rigid ones, the unstable ones, each labeled with what was pushed too far. This is the kind of failure people need to see, not just read about.

## Why it happens, and how to stop fighting it

The fix is almost always to stop pushing so hard. A cleaner dataset means the model does not have to be forced. Training to the sweet spot instead of past it keeps you in the zone where the model bends willingly. Lowering strength at generation time eases the strain. The theme of the whole module returns one more time: **work with the model, not against it.** Force is the beginner's instinct, and it is the thing that breaks results.
