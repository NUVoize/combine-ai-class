# Lesson 2.11: The Pollution Factor

This is one of the most important ideas in the whole local path, and one almost nobody teaches by name. When you attach a LoRA, it does not sit in a tidy box, adding your character and touching nothing else. It reshapes the base model, and that reshaping spills into places you did not intend. Call it **the pollution factor**: the way a LoRA bleeds beyond its job and colors the rest of what the model makes. Understand this and a hundred mysterious results suddenly make sense.

## What is actually happening

Back in Lesson 2.8 we said a LoRA is a focused nudge to a model that already knows almost everything. The catch is in the word *nudge*. To make room for your character, training has to push on the model's existing knowledge, and the model's knowledge is all tangled together. So pushing on "your character" inevitably leans on nearby concepts. The model does not have a clean, separate drawer for one person. Teaching it your character subtly tugs on everything connected to that person: faces in general, the body type, the style, the settings that showed up in your dataset.

> **The LoRA does not only add your character. It tilts the whole model toward the world your dataset showed it.**

## How pollution shows up

You will recognize it once you know to look:

- **Bleed into other subjects.** Other people in your images start drifting toward your character's features.
- **Baked-in surroundings.** Backgrounds, lighting, or clothing that were common in your dataset start appearing even when you did not ask (closely tied to the captioning lesson, 2.7).
- **A shift in overall look.** The model's general style leans toward the flavor of your training images.
- **Reduced range.** The model gets a little worse at things far from your dataset, because you pulled it toward your character.

## Why this is your edge to teach

Most people feel pollution without ever naming it. They get strange results and blame the wrong thing. Because you have trained close to a thousand LoRAs, you have seen every flavor of this, and explaining it clearly is exactly the kind of hard-won insight that sets this course apart. This is a place to go deep.

## How to keep pollution in check

The short version, to be expanded with your detail: a clean, well-captioned, balanced dataset pollutes less. Training to the sweet spot rather than overtraining pollutes less. And at generation time, you can dial a LoRA's strength down so it colors less. The next lesson is about what happens when you push in the other direction, hard, and the model pushes back.
