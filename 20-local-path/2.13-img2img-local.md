# Lesson 2.13: Image-to-Image, When and Why to Reach for It

You met image-to-image in the Cloud Path (Lesson 1.6), where it was your main way to carry a character. Now you have a LoRA that locks identity on its own, so a fair question is: why use image-to-image at all anymore? The answer is that they solve *different* problems, and the best local work uses both. Your LoRA controls **who**. Image-to-image controls the **specific image you start from**: the composition, the pose, the structure. This lesson is about reaching for the second one with intent.

## What image-to-image gives you that a LoRA does not

A LoRA puts your character in the model, but it does not tell the model *what specific picture to make*. Prompt alone for the composition and you are back to rolling dice on framing and pose. Image-to-image fixes that: you hand the model a starting image and it builds from that exact layout. So:

- **LoRA:** the right person.
- **Image-to-image:** that person, in *this* exact arrangement.

Different jobs, and together they are far stronger than either alone.

## Same mechanic, now fully yours

The mechanic is identical to Lesson 1.6, including the **strength dial** that trades fidelity-to-source against freedom-to-change. What is different is that you now run it on your own model, with your own LoRA attached, with no external limits and finer control. The fiddly cloud version becomes a precise local instrument.

## When to reach for it

- You need a **specific pose or composition**, not whatever the prompt rolls.
- You are **refining an existing image** rather than starting fresh.
- You want to **keep a structure** (a layout, a silhouette) while changing the content or style.
- You are setting up to **stack it with your LoRA**, which is the next lesson.

## The trap to avoid

Do not let image-to-image drag in things you did not want, the same carry-over warning from Lesson 1.6 (pose, lighting, and background hitch a ride). With a LoRA in play you have more control, but the discipline is the same: know what you are taking from the source image and what you are leaving behind.
