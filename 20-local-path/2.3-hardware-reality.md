# Lesson 2.3: Hardware Reality, What Each Model Tier Actually Demands

This is the lesson nobody likes but everyone needs. Local power runs on hardware, and the single number that decides most of what you can do is **VRAM**, the memory on your graphics card. Not your regular RAM, not your processor, the GPU's own memory. This lesson sets honest expectations so you do not buy the wrong thing or waste days fighting a model your machine cannot run.

## The one number that matters most: VRAM

Running a model and *training* on a model are two different weights, and training is the heavier one. A card that generates images comfortably may not have the memory to train a LoRA on the same family. So when you read a requirement, always ask: is this for generating, or for training? Training is the bar that matters here.

## The honest tiers

Without naming specific cards yet, the landscape sorts into a few bands:

- **Entry:** runs lighter families, generates fine, but training is slow or out of reach. A real starting point, with real limits.
- **Mid:** the comfortable middle. Trains the common families without too much pain. Where most serious hobbyists land.
- **High end:** trains the heavy families and runs everything. Expensive, and not required to do excellent work.

## What about the rest of the machine

VRAM dominates, but a few other things matter at the margins: system RAM, fast storage for datasets and checkpoints, and a processor that will not bottleneck you. None of these are the deciding factor, and you should not let them distract you from the VRAM question.

## If your machine cannot do it

Here is the relief valve, and it changes the whole calculation: you do not actually need to *own* the hardware. You can rent a powerful GPU by the hour and train on a machine far beyond anything you own. That option is the entire next lesson, and it means a modest setup is no barrier to doing serious local work.
