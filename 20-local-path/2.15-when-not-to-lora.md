# Lesson 2.15: When NOT to Use a LoRA at All

We end the local path on judgment, because that is what separates someone who *can* train a LoRA from someone who knows when they *should*. A LoRA is the most powerful tool in this course, and like any powerful tool it is the wrong choice surprisingly often. Reaching for one out of habit costs you time and invites side effects you did not need. This lesson is about knowing when to leave it in the box.

## A LoRA has a real cost

Before training one, be honest about what it asks: a properly built dataset (Module 2B), a training run and the hardware or rental for it (Module 2A), the under/over-training tightrope (Lesson 2.9), and the side effects of pollution and strain (Lessons 2.11 and 2.12). That is a lot of investment. It pays off beautifully for a character you will use heavily. It is wildly overkill for a face you need once.

## When to skip the LoRA

Reach for a lighter tool when:

- **The character appears once or twice.** A prompt plus image-to-image from a good reference (the entire Cloud Path) is faster and good enough.
- **You do not have a strong dataset.** A LoRA from a weak dataset is worse than no LoRA. If you cannot feed it well, do not train it.
- **You only need loose consistency.** If "close enough" is genuinely close enough, the cloud workflow already does that without a training run.
- **The job is a one-off composition.** Image-to-image and control tools can place a generic character in a specific shot without anyone needing to be locked.
- **The side effects would cost more than the benefit.** If pollution or strain would hurt the rest of your work, a lighter approach keeps the model clean.

## When the LoRA is right

Train one when the character is a **recurring lead** you will generate many times, when you need identity **truly locked** across lots of varied scenes, and when **usually-close stopped being enough** (the exact moment Lesson 1.9 told you to wait for). That is what a LoRA is for. For everything below that bar, lighter tools win.

## The real skill you have built

Step back and see what you can do now. You can steer a model from the outside and lock a character from the inside. You can build datasets, train LoRAs, control composition, and stack it all together. And now you can look at any job and choose the *lightest tool that does it*, which is the mark of someone who actually understands this craft rather than just operating it. Matching the tool to the job has been the through-line since Lesson 1.3, and here it becomes second nature.
