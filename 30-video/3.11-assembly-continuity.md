# Lesson 3.11: Assembling Clips into a Coherent Scene

You have generated your shots (Lesson 3.9). Right now they are a folder of separate clips. This lesson is about the last step that turns them into an actual scene: assembly. This is editing, the craft of ordering shots, timing the cuts, and smoothing the seams so a viewer feels one continuous moment instead of a sequence of AI clips. It is the back half of the cinema skill, and it is where a scene finally comes alive.

## From a pile of clips to a scene

The breakdown gave you an intended order (Lesson 3.8). Assembly is where you commit to it and refine:

- **Order the shots** along your scene's beats, establishing to coverage to the moments that matter.
- **Time the cuts.** When to hold a shot, when to cut, how pacing carries the feeling of the scene. Pure craft, and pure judgment.
- **Smooth the seams** so cuts feel motivated and continuous, not random.

## Fixing continuity in the edit

No matter how carefully you generated, small mismatches slip through: a lighting jump between shots, a slight color difference, a tiny identity wobble at the edge of a clip. The edit is where you catch and fix these:

- **Trim around drift.** If a clip drifts late, cut away before it gets bad. Short, clean pieces beat long, drifting ones, the Lesson 3.4 logic applied in the edit.
- **Match color and light** across shots so the scene feels like one space.
- **Hide seams on motion or action**, the classic editor's trick for making a cut invisible.

## A note on sound

Sound is half of film, and it is largely its own discipline. It is worth flagging that a finished scene usually needs audio to feel complete, even if the full treatment lives beyond this course.
