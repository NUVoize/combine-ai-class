# Lesson 3.1: Why Video Is a Harder Problem

Everything up to now has been about a single still image: getting the same character twice, then a hundred times, then locking them into a model you trained yourself. Video is the same spine, extended into motion. The goal has not changed, it is still character consistency. What changes is that consistency now has to hold not just from image to image, but across every single frame of a moving clip, and those frames have to flow together believably.

That is a genuinely harder problem, and this lesson is about why.

## The new dimension: time

A video is just many images per second, played in sequence. That one fact creates two new problems on top of everything you already know:

1. **Identity has to hold across every frame.** If your character's face shifts even slightly from frame to frame, you do not get a still drift you can reroll, you get a face that *shimmers and morphs* while it moves. The wobble becomes motion.
2. **The frames have to cohere over time.** Beyond the face, the whole world has to stay stable: lighting that does not flicker, a background that does not swim, motion that flows instead of stutters. This is called temporal coherence, and it is a problem still images simply do not have.

So video is image consistency *plus* motion coherence, stacked on top of each other.

## Why this is the top of the mountain

More frames means more chances to drift, and the model is not only holding an identity now, it is also inventing believable movement. Two hard jobs at once. This is why video sits at the top of the course, it asks for everything you learned in the still world and then adds time.

> **[FILL LATER: drift-in-motion example]** A short clip where the face shimmers and the background swims, next to a stable one. Seeing identity wobble *in motion* lands harder than any still example.

## The good news

You are not starting over. The character you learned to lock in Level 2 is the foundation for all of this, and the video tools build on the exact same ideas you already know: feed the visual information in, condition or train on your character, and control the result rather than hoping. The spine carries straight through.
