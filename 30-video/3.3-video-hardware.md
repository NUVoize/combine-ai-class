# Lesson 3.3: Hardware and Requirements for Video

Video is heavier than stills in every direction: more compute, more memory, longer waits, and hard caps on how long and how large a clip can be. This lesson sets honest expectations so you plan around the limits instead of slamming into them mid-project.

## Why video costs so much more

A still is one image. A few seconds of video is dozens or hundreds of images that also have to agree with each other over time (Lesson 3.1). That multiplies everything: the memory to hold it, the compute to generate it, and the time you wait. Where a still renders in seconds, a clip can take minutes, and a long or high-resolution clip can be out of reach entirely on modest hardware.

## What this means in practice

- **Clip length is limited.** Most tools generate short clips, then you assemble many of them (Module 3D). Plan in short shots, not long takes.
- **Resolution and frame rate cost you.** Higher of either means more compute and tighter limits.
- **Generation is slow.** Iteration is more expensive than in the still world, so planning before you generate matters far more (which is exactly what the cinema module is about).

## Cloud, local, or rented

The same three options from Level 2, now skewed harder toward not owning the hardware:

- **Cloud video tools** are where most people work, because the compute is enormous.
- **Rented GPUs** (the RunPods idea from Lesson 2.4) let you run heavier local video pipelines without owning the cards.
- **Local on your own machine** is possible for lighter setups but demands serious hardware for anything ambitious.

## The mindset shift

Because video is slow and capped, the cheap-iteration habits of the still world do not transfer. You cannot afford to roll a hundred clips and pick one. This forces a more deliberate, planned approach, and that planning discipline is exactly the professional skill the next module is built on.
