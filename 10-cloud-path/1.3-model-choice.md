# Lesson 1.3: Picking the Right Model for the Job

You now have the buckets (Lesson 1.1) and the tools (Lesson 1.2). This short lesson is the skill that ties them together: choosing. Most beginner frustration is not a skill gap, it is a tool-choice error. People reach for a generation model to hold a character and then blame themselves when the face wanders. The face was always going to wander. It was the wrong bucket.

So let's make choosing automatic.

## The only question that matters

Before you open anything, ask yourself one thing:

> **Am I inventing, or am I protecting?**

That is the whole decision, and it falls straight out of Lesson 0.5. If you are inventing something brand new, words are perfect, because you *want* the model's imagination filling the gaps. If you are protecting an identity that already exists, words are the wrong tool, because they cannot carry that identity and will quietly swap it out.

Inventing leans on generation. Protecting leans on edit or hybrid. Every time.

## Matching the job to the bucket

Here is the same idea spelled out for the situations you will actually hit:

- **"I need a brand-new character, concept, or empty background I have never seen."** Generation. There is no identity to protect yet, so let the model invent freely.
- **"I have a character and need them in a new scene, pose, or outfit."** Edit or hybrid. You are protecting an identity, so the actual visual information has to go in, not a description of it.
- **"I need one specific change to an existing image."** Remove an object, change a jacket from blue to red, fix an awkward hand. Edit. This is surgical work, and edit models are the scalpel.
- **"I want to iterate, generate something and then keep adjusting that same result."** Hybrid, because it keeps you in one window instead of exporting and re-importing between tools.

## The trade-offs, in one breath

Each bucket buys you something and costs you something:

- **Generation** gives you maximum invention and minimum control over identity.
- **Edit** gives you maximum control over one specific image and less free creativity.
- **Hybrid** gives you the smooth middle, generate and edit in one place, at the small price of being slightly less specialized than a dedicated tool at either extreme.

None of these is better. They are different jobs.

> **[FILL LATER: worked decision example]** Take one real goal (for example, "I have a portrait of my character and I want her standing in a rainy street at night") and walk the choice out loud, ending at edit or hybrid, with the actual screens.

## The three mistakes to avoid

1. **Prompting your way to the same face in a generation model.** This is the Lesson 0.5 trap. No stack of adjectives pins one identity. If you catch yourself adding more and more description to "lock" a face, stop, you are in the wrong bucket.
2. **Reaching for a heavy edit tool for a throwaway idea.** If you are just exploring concepts, generation is faster and looser. Save the careful tools for when identity is on the line.
3. **Switching tools mid-iteration.** Every hop loses your thread. When you are refining one character, stay in one hybrid so each result feeds the next.

The cheat line for all of it: **invent in generation, protect in edit or hybrid.**
