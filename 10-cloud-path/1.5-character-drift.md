# Lesson 1.5: Character Drift, and the Habits That Fight It

We have named this enemy a few times. Now we look straight at it and build a working routine to push back.

**Character drift** is the identity sliding from image to image: the face that was sharp in the first render softens in the second, the nose changes, and by the fifth image you are looking at a cousin, then a stranger. You know exactly why by now. The model has no memory (Lesson 0.3), it builds each image fresh from noise, and your words cannot carry the face (Lesson 0.5). Drift is not a bug in your prompt. It is the default behavior of the medium.

Here is the honest framing for this whole lesson: **you cannot eliminate drift with prompting alone.** That is what reference images and, later, training your own models are for. But you *can* reduce it a great deal with discipline, and a disciplined prompt-only workflow is often good enough for casual work. This lesson is that discipline.

## The habits

**1. Change one variable at a time.** Hold the identity block byte for byte identical and only touch the scene block (Lesson 1.4). If you reword the identity on every render, you are hand-feeding the model permission to drift.

**2. Reuse a fixed identity block.** Copy and paste it. Do not retype it from memory, because small wording changes you will not even notice ("dark hair" one day, "black hair" the next) are enough to shift the result.

**3. Control the seed on purpose.** Recall Lesson 0.4: the seed is not an identity, but it is a source of variation. Lock it while you are comparing two prompts, so you are comparing the *wording* and not just two different dice rolls. Unlock it when you actually want variety. Stay clear-eyed though: same seed with a changed prompt still drifts, because the seed was never the face.

**4. Do not over-specify.** As in Lesson 1.4, every extra adjective is one more thing the model juggles, and juggling makes it drop traits. A tight identity block drifts less than a bloated one.

**5. Give the eye an anchor.** A distinctive, stable feature, a specific jacket, round glasses, a scar, reads as "same person" even when the underlying face shifts a little. It is cheap, and it covers for a surprising amount of mild drift. Pick a signature and keep it in the identity block.

**6. Generate in batches and select.** Make several versions, pick the one closest to your character, and carry *that* one forward. Selection is not cheating, it is a core technique. The image you choose becomes the reference for everything next.

**7. Stay in a hybrid so your best result can feed the next one.** The moment you have a keeper, a hybrid lets you hand it straight back in rather than describing it again (Lesson 1.1). This is the on-ramp to the next module.

## What these habits actually buy you

Used together, these turn pure-prompt character work from "a new person every time" into "recognizably the same person, with some wobble." For a lot of casual and hobby work, that is enough.

But be honest with yourself about the ceiling. **These habits manage drift, they do not solve it.** They are you working around a gap, not closing it. The actual close is to stop describing the face and start handing it in as a picture, which is the next module and the real heart of the cloud path.
