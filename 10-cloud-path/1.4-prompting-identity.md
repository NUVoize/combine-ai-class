# Lesson 1.4: Prompting for Identity, What Text Can and Cannot Carry

This is the lesson where Module 0 starts paying you back. You already know the deep reason a face wanders: a low-bandwidth text control is driving a high-bandwidth visual output (Lesson 0.5). So instead of asking your prompt to do the one thing it physically cannot, we are going to point it at the things it does brilliantly, and hand the hard part to other tools in the next lessons.

The trick is knowing which is which.

## What text carries well

Words are excellent at *crowd-level* description, anything that sorts the world into broad groups:

- Age range, build, and overall vibe
- Hair color and length, broad coloring
- Wardrobe and props ("a worn leather jacket," "round glasses")
- Setting, time of day, weather
- Lighting and mood ("soft window light," "harsh neon")
- Framing and composition ("close-up," "wide shot, low angle")
- Art style ("35mm film," "flat illustration")

For all of this, text is the right tool. You are describing a category, and the model lands inside that category reliably.

## What text cannot carry

What words cannot do is pin one specific face. The exact distance between the eyes, the precise curve of the nose, the hundred tiny proportions that make a face *that* face and not a sibling's (Lesson 0.5). No adjective stack reaches that resolution. "Green eyes, brown hair, sharp jaw" still describes millions of different people, and the model picks a different one each run.

So here is the reframe that makes prompting useful again:

> **Use the prompt to land in the right neighborhood, then stop.**

Your words get you a person who *could* be your character, a strong, repeatable starting point. They were never going to be the identity system. The identity gets locked by feeding the actual face in, which is the next module.

## Split your prompt into two blocks

A simple habit makes this concrete. Write every prompt in two parts:

- **The identity block.** The crowd-level traits plus a wardrobe signature. This stays *identical* every time, copied and pasted, never retyped.
- **The scene block.** Setting, pose, lighting, action. This is the part you change from image to image.

For example:

- **Identity block:** *a woman in her late twenties, short black hair, sharp features, a worn green military jacket*
- **Scene block (image 1):** *standing on a rainy city street at night, neon reflections, wide shot*
- **Scene block (image 2):** *sitting in a sunlit cafe, soft morning light, close-up*

Same person, two scenes, and you only touched the second block.

## Tight beats verbose

One counterintuitive rule: do not over-describe. Every extra adjective is another constraint the model has to balance, and past a point it starts trading one trait off against another to satisfy them all. A short, concrete identity block holds together better than a long one stuffed with detail. Say the few things that matter and stop.

## The honest limit

Even a perfect identity block will still drift in a pure generation model, because words still cannot carry the face. That is not a failure of your prompt, it is the ceiling we have been describing since Lesson 0.5. Prompting well makes everything downstream easier, but it is the setup, not the solve.
