# Lesson 2.7: Captioning, Balance, and the Common Mistakes

You have clean, well-spread images (Lessons 2.5 and 2.6). Now you tell the model what it is looking at. **Captioning** is writing a short text description for each training image, and it is the most misunderstood step in the whole process. Get it right and your character is flexible and obedient. Get it wrong and it is rigid, or it bleeds into everything you generate. This lesson is where good datasets become good *training*.

## What captioning actually does

When the model trains, it links what it sees in each image to the words in that image's caption. So your captions decide which parts of each image become "the character" and which parts stay adjustable. This is the lever that controls how your trained character behaves later, and almost nobody explains it clearly.

The core principle, said plainly:

> **What you caption becomes variable. What you leave uncaptioned gets baked into the character.**

If you caption the red jacket, the model learns "the jacket is a separate, changeable thing." If you never mention it, the model assumes the jacket is *part of who this character is* and starts attaching it to them everywhere. That one idea explains most captioning successes and failures.

> **[FILL LATER: caption walkthrough]** Two or three real training images with your actual captions written out, annotated to show what you chose to describe and what you deliberately left out, and why.

## Balance

Balance is making sure your dataset does not accidentally over-teach one thing. If eighty percent of your images are the same pose, or the same background, or the same expression, the model over-learns it and drags it into everything. Balance means spreading your images so no single incidental feature dominates. It works hand in hand with the variety rule from Lesson 2.5: variety in the images, balance in their distribution.

> **[FILL LATER: your captioning and balance system]** Your real method: caption style and length, what you always caption versus always omit, the tool you use, and how you check balance. This is signature, hard-won craft, put your stamp on it.

## The common mistakes

The same handful of errors sink most first attempts:

- **Over-captioning** the character's own defining features, so the identity never locks.
- **Under-captioning** incidental clutter, so junk gets baked in.
- **Inconsistent captions** across the set, so the model gets mixed signals.
- **Imbalanced sets** that over-teach one pose, angle, or background.

> **[FILL LATER: before/after fixes]** For each mistake, the broken result and the fixed one. Showing the failure is more convincing than describing it.
