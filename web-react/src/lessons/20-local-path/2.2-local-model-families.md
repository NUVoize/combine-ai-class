# Lesson 2.2: The Local Model Families and How They Differ

Before you install anything or think about hardware, you need a map of what you are choosing between. Local image models come in families, and they are not interchangeable. The family you pick decides how you build datasets, how you train, what hardware you need, and what your results look like. Choosing well here saves you from redoing everything later.

This lesson is the map. We are not chasing the newest release, the same way Lesson 1.2 taught you to *read* a tool rather than memorize a list. New models land constantly. What stays stable is how to tell families apart and what each one asks of you.

## How to tell families apart

For any local model family, four things matter:

1. **Architecture and generation.** Which base family it belongs to, and roughly how modern it is. This drives everything downstream.
2. **What it is good at.** Photorealism, stylization, anatomy, prompt understanding, speed. Every family has a personality, same as the cloud tools.
3. **What it demands.** How much VRAM and time it takes to run and, more importantly, to *train* on. This is where families separate hard.
4. **Ecosystem and tooling.** How much community support, how many existing LoRAs and tools, how well documented. A slightly weaker model with a rich ecosystem often beats a stronger one with none.

> **[FILL LATER: the family map]** The actual roster of model families you teach with, each as a short profile: generation, strengths, training demand, ecosystem, and your recommendation for who should use it. Lead with the one or two you actually train on most. Keep it opinionated.

## The same rule from Lesson 1.3 applies here

Everything from Lesson 1.3 carries straight into the local world: there is no do-it-all model. Families differ in what they are built for, a hyperreal look, a cinematic look, a stylized one, and the family you pick should be driven by the end goal you are after, not by which one is "best" in the abstract. Expect to keep more than one family within reach, because each has a blind spot the others cover.

## The trade you are really making

Across families, the pattern is almost always the same: **newer and more capable usually means heavier to run and train.** A more powerful family gives better results but demands more VRAM, more time, and often a rented GPU. A lighter family is easier to live with but tops out sooner. There is no free lunch, only the right match for your hardware and your goals.

> **[FILL LATER: side-by-side outputs]** The same character or prompt rendered across the families you cover, so students can see the personality differences with their own eyes.

## Why this comes before hardware

You cannot sensibly choose hardware until you know which family you are committing to, because the model sets the requirement, not the other way around. So fix your family first. The next lesson turns that choice into a concrete hardware reality.
