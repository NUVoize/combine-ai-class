# Lesson 1.3: Picking Your Approach (There Is No Do-It-All Model)

You have the buckets (Lesson 1.1) and the tools (Lesson 1.2). This lesson is the judgment that ties them together, and it starts with a question most people skip: *what are you actually trying to make?* The honest answer changes everything downstream, not just which model you open, but the whole technique you use to get there.

## Start from the end goal, not the tool

Before you pick a model, picture the finished result. A hyperreal portrait that looks like a real person on Instagram, a cinematic frame that feels like a film still, a clean stylized illustration, a product shot: these are completely different targets. And here is the part beginners miss: **they do not just call for different models, they call for different techniques.** The road to a convincing real-person look is not the road to a cinematic look, even when the character is the same. Pick the target first, then build the approach around it.

> **[FILL LATER: target gallery]** The same character pulled toward different end goals (hyperreal, cinematic, stylized), each noting the different model and technique used. Seeing one character pulled toward different targets makes the point instantly.

## Two questions, not one

Model choice really runs on two questions stacked together.

**1. What am I going for?** The target look and the kind of scene. This points you at a *family* of models and techniques suited to that aesthetic.

**2. Am I inventing or protecting?** Within that family, this is the job question. If you are inventing something brand new, generation is fine, words are doing exactly what they are good at (Lesson 0.5). If you are protecting an identity that already exists, you reach for edit or hybrid, because text cannot carry a face.

Answer both, in that order, and the right tool is usually obvious.

## The goal dictates the technique, not just the model

This is the deeper half, and the part almost nobody teaches. Two creators can open the very same model and get wildly different results, because their *technique* is aimed at different goals. A real-person look leans on certain choices: skin and lighting realism, careful reference handling, restrained refinement. A cinematic look leans on others: framing, lens feel, mood, grade. The scene pushes it further still, a short static shot, a long take, and a fast action beat each ask for a different approach, which is something the video level (Level 3) leans on heavily. **Your end goal is the real instruction. The model is just one of the tools serving it.**

## The big truth: no model does it all

Here is the rule that outranks every tool recommendation in this course:

> **There is no do-it-all model. Every model has a blind spot.**

Some models are excellent generalists and will carry most of what you throw at them. That is genuinely useful, and for a lot of work a strong generalist is enough. But if you want the best of the best, you have to be able to use and understand *several* models, because the moment you push toward the edge of what one does well, it fails, and a different model is suddenly the right answer. Mastery here is not finding the one perfect tool. It is building a small stable of tools and knowing exactly where each one breaks.

This is why the course teaches multiple roads and multiple models instead of selling you a single magic button. The skill is the judgment, not the tool.

## The mistakes to avoid

1. **Picking the tool before the goal.** Choose a model first and then decide what to make, and you end up bending your idea to fit the tool. Decide the target, then pick.
2. **Expecting one model to nail every look.** When a model struggles at a target it was never strong at, that is not your failure, it is the wrong tool for that goal. Switch.
3. **Prompting harder instead of switching.** Piling more words onto a model that is blind to what you want will not fix it. Reach for the model that can see it.

The cheat line for the whole lesson: **start from the end goal, match the model and the technique to it, and expect to switch tools as the goal changes.**
