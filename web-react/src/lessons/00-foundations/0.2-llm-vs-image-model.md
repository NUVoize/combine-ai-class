# Lesson 0.2: LLMs vs. Image Models, Same Kind of Brain, Different Output

You have used ChatGPT or Claude. You are now using an image generator. It is natural to assume they are completely different kinds of machines, one does words, one does pictures. But under the hood they are far more alike than they look, and understanding *how* they are alike hands you the key insight that the rest of this course turns into technique.

## Two machines, one habit: predict and sample

A chat model like Claude does one thing, over and over: it predicts the next chunk of text. You give it some words, it asks "what most likely comes next?", produces a piece, adds it on, and asks again. Word by word, that is how a whole paragraph gets built. It is never *recalling* a stored sentence, it is generating one piece at a time by sampling from likelihoods.

An image model does the exact same style of thing, just aimed at pixels instead of words. As we covered in the last lesson, it starts from noise and repeatedly predicts "how do I clean up this static a little to move it closer to the picture being described?" Step by step, sampling as it goes, an image emerges.

Different output, text vs. image, but the same fundamental habit: **predict, sample, repeat.** Neither one is a database you are querying. Both are generators producing something new each time.

## The property that matters most: they do not remember

Here is the part to burn in. Both kinds of model are **stateless**, they have no built-in memory of what came before.

This surprises people about chat models too. When you have a long back-and-forth with Claude and it seems to "remember" what you said five messages ago, the model itself is not remembering anything. The app is quietly pasting the entire conversation back in front of it every single time you hit send. Take that re-pasting away and the model is a blank slate, it has no idea you two have ever spoken.

The image model is in exactly the same boat, except there is usually nothing pasting the past back in. Each generation stands alone, knowing nothing about the last one.

## The insight you will use for the rest of the course

Sit with the chat example for a second, because it contains the entire solution to the character problem in miniature.

How does a stateless, memoryless chat model manage to hold a coherent conversation? **Because the app keeps feeding the relevant history back into it.** The memory does not live in the model, it lives in what you put *in front of* the model each time.

That is precisely the trick for consistent characters. The model will never carry your character forward on its own, because it cannot carry anything forward. So the entire game becomes: **how do I feed the character back in, every time, so the model is looking at it while it generates?**

Training datasets, edit models, and the rest of the toolkit ahead are all different, increasingly powerful ways of feeding the character back in front of a model that would otherwise start every image as a total stranger.

You now know what kind of machine you are driving. Next we will make the no-memory problem concrete, and then look at the first thing everyone *thinks* will solve it, seeds, and why it does not.
