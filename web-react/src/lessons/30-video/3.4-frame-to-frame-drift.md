# Lesson 3.4: Frame-to-Frame Drift, the New Enemy

You know drift from the still world: ask for the same character twice and get a cousin (Lesson 0.5). Video has its own version, and it is meaner, because it happens *inside a single clip*, in motion, where you cannot just reroll one frame. This lesson names it so you can recognize it and fight it.

## The two failures

Video drift shows up in two distinct ways, and it helps to keep them separate:

1. **Identity drift across frames.** Your character's face changes as the clip plays. The nose shifts, the proportions slide, and what should be one person in motion becomes a face that morphs and shimmers. This is Lesson 0.5's gap, now smeared across time.
2. **Temporal incoherence.** Everything *around* the identity fails to hold: the background swims or warps, lighting flickers, textures crawl, motion stutters or melts. The world will not stay still even when the face does.

A good clip needs both solved at once. Either failure alone breaks it.

## Why it happens

Same root cause as always. The model has no persistent lock on your character (Lesson 0.3), and now it is generating many frames that each have a chance to wander, while *also* having to invent believable motion. More frames, more drift opportunities, plus a second hard job stacked on top. That is why video is the top of the mountain.

## The habits that fight it (preview)

The full techniques are the next lessons, but here is the shape:

- **Start from a strong, locked keyframe** (image-to-video from a correct character still). A right first frame anchors everything after it.
- **Keep clips short.** Drift compounds over time, so shorter clips drift less. This also fits the hardware limits from Lesson 3.3.
- **Limit the motion.** Big, fast movement gives the model more room to lose the thread. Calmer motion holds better.
- **Use whatever consistency or conditioning controls the tool offers**, and lean on your Level 2 character work as the source.
