# Lesson 3.2: The Video Model Families

Just like still tools (Lessons 1.2 and 2.2), video tools come in families, and the family decides how you drive it. The same lesson applies: this space moves fast, so we learn to *read* a video tool, not memorize a list that will be stale in a month.

## The three ways a video model is driven

- **Text-to-video (t2v).** A prompt goes in, a clip comes out, built from nothing. This is the video cousin of text-to-image: maximum invention, minimum identity control. Great for fresh shots where no specific character has to be protected, and exactly the wrong tool the moment identity matters (the Lesson 0.5 trap, now in motion).
- **Image-to-video (i2v).** You hand the model a starting image and it animates it into a clip. **This is the key family for character work.** Start from a still of your locked character and bring it to life, identity carried in from the frame instead of described in words. If you remember one family from this lesson, remember this one.
- **Video-to-video (v2v).** An existing clip goes in and the model transforms it, keeping the motion and structure while changing the look. Useful for restyling and for driving a character with real reference motion.

## Reading any video tool

The four-question lens from Lesson 1.2, adapted for video:

1. **Can I feed in an image or a video, or only words?** This sorts it into t2v, i2v, or v2v immediately.
2. **What does it do with what I feed it?** Does it faithfully animate my frame, or just take loose inspiration?
3. **What is it good at?** Realism, motion quality, length, resolution, prompt control.
4. **What are its limits and costs?** Clip length, resolution caps, generation time, price. Video limits bite harder than image ones.

> **[FILL LATER: video tool roster]** The editorial list, same as Lesson 1.2 but for video. For each tool you teach: name, family (t2v / i2v / v2v), what it is great at, where it drifts, clip length and resolution limits, and access and cost. Lead with the i2v tool you use most for character work.

## The rule that carries over

The same principle from the still world holds: **invent in text-to-video, protect identity in image-to-video.** When the character matters, you start from a frame of *your* character, you do not describe them and hope.

And the bigger rule from Lesson 1.3 carries too: there is no do-it-all video model. The look you want, plus the length of the clip and the kind of action, push you toward different models and different techniques. Short, calm, realistic motion and long, stylized, high-action sequences are not the same job, and no single tool is best at both. Pick your video model from the goal, and keep more than one within reach.
