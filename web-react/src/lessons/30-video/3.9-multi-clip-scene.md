# Lesson 3.9: Preparing and Generating a Multi-Clip Scene

You have a shot list from the breakdown (Lesson 3.8). Now you execute it: prepare the assets each shot needs, generate the clips, and hold continuity across all of them so they read as one scene and not a collection of strangers in similar lighting. This is where everything in the course comes together into real production.

## Prepare before you generate

Because video is slow and capped (Lesson 3.3), preparation is most of the work. For each shot on your list:

- **Make the start frame.** Generate the locked character still the shot opens from, using your Level 2 character (Lesson 3.5). Same character source across every shot, this is the backbone of continuity.
- **Lock the look.** Decide the lighting, environment, wardrobe, and tone, and keep them consistent shot to shot. A character who is correct but lit differently in every clip still breaks the scene.
- **Write the shot's prompt and motion plan** from the breakdown, so you are not improvising at the tool.

## Generate with continuity discipline

Continuity is the whole game in a multi-clip scene. The watchwords:

- **One character source** feeding every shot, so identity matches across cuts.
- **Consistent environment and lighting** so the space feels like one place.
- **Matching screen direction and eyelines** so cuts do not disorient.
- **Generate, then select.** Make several takes of each shot and keep the best, exactly the batch-and-select habit from Lesson 1.5, now per shot. Selection is a core part of the craft, not a shortcut.

## When a shot will not cooperate

Some shots fight you, too much motion, drift you cannot tame, an action the tool cannot do (Lesson 3.4). The cinema answer is to *re-stage*, not to brute-force. Break the shot into two simpler ones, change the angle, reduce the motion, or cut around the hard moment. Knowing how to re-stage around a limitation is, again, exactly the craft your background brings.
