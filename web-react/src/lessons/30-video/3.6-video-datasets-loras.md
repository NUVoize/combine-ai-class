# Lesson 3.6: Datasets and LoRAs for Video

In the still world, the most powerful way to lock a character was to train the model itself (Module 2C). The same idea exists for video, and it is the strongest tool here too, but it is also the most demanding and the fastest-moving corner of this whole field. This lesson covers the concept and where it fits, and leaves the specifics to fill, because the tooling here changes faster than anywhere else in the course.

## The same spine, one more time

The principle has not changed since Lesson 0.5: the deepest fix is to teach the model your character so it carries the identity itself, rather than describing it or handing in a single frame. For video, that can mean training on your character for use in a video model, so motion generates with the identity already baked in, rather than relying only on the start-frame trick from Lesson 3.5.

## What changes from still training

Everything you learned in Module 2B and 2C still frames your thinking, but video adds weight:

- **The data can be heavier.** Some video training wants more than still images, and that raises the bar on preparation and hardware.
- **The demands go up.** More compute, more memory, longer training. Rented GPUs (Lesson 2.4) matter even more here.
- **The tooling is younger.** Video training methods are evolving quickly, so the exact tools and best practices shift more than they do for still LoRAs.

> **[FILL LATER: video training approach]** Your current method for training or conditioning a character into video, the tools, the data, the settings, and the realistic expectations. Flag this as the most likely lesson to need updating as the field moves.

## When it is worth it

The judgment from Lesson 2.15 carries straight over. Training for video is worth it for a **recurring lead** you will put through many scenes and need genuinely locked in motion. For a one-off shot, the start-frame image-to-video method (Lesson 3.5) is far less effort and usually enough. Do not train a video model for a character who appears once.

> **[FILL LATER: trained vs start-frame comparison]** The same character in motion, once via start-frame image-to-video and once via a trained approach, so students can see what the extra effort buys and decide for themselves.
