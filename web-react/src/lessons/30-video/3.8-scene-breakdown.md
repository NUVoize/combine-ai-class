# Lesson 3.8: Breaking Down a Scene, Shot by Shot

A scene breakdown is taking a scene you can picture in your head and decomposing it into the actual list of shots you need to generate, in order, with a clear plan for each. This is standard practice in real filmmaking, and bringing it to AI video is what separates a planned scene from a pile of lucky clips. It is also where the slow, expensive nature of video generation (Lesson 3.3) pays you back: an hour of planning saves a day of rerolling.

## What a breakdown produces

By the end of breaking down a scene, you should have a concrete shot list. For each shot, you know roughly:

- **What it shows** (the subject and the action).
- **The shot type and angle** (wide establishing, medium, close-up, and so on).
- **What it needs to generate** (which character still or keyframe, what prompt, how much motion).
- **How it connects** to the shots before and after (continuity of place, lighting, eyeline, direction).

That list is your generation plan. You are no longer improvising at the tool, you are executing a plan you already trust.

## The cinema craft behind it

Deciding *which* shots a moment actually needs, and in what order, is the real skill, and it is entirely your domain. Coverage, pacing, when to cut to a close-up, how to establish a space, how to keep screen direction consistent: these are craft decisions a prompt cannot teach.

## How AI changes the breakdown (and how it does not)

The craft of breakdown is unchanged from traditional film. What AI adds is a new column of practical questions per shot: can the tool actually generate this shot, what start frame does it need, how long can it be, and how much motion can it hold before drifting (Lesson 3.4). So an AI breakdown is the classic shot list plus a feasibility pass. You plan the scene like a filmmaker, then sanity-check each shot against what the tools can do.
