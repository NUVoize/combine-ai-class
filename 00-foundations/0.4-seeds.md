# Lesson 0.4: Seeds, What They Are, and Why They Will Not Save Your Character

Sooner or later every beginner has the same hopeful thought: *"If the randomness is the problem, I will just lock the randomness. I will find the magic seed that makes my character, and reuse it forever."* It is a smart instinct. It is also a dead end for character work, and understanding exactly why is one of the most clarifying moments in this whole module.

## What a seed actually is

Remember from Lesson 0.1 that every image begins as a field of pure random static. Well, that static has to come from somewhere, and computers cannot truly invent randomness out of nothing. So they use a starting number, a **seed**, to generate that initial field of noise. Same seed, same starting static. Different seed, different starting static.

That is all a seed is: **the number that decides which exact field of random noise you start from.** It is not a style, not a character, not a setting with any meaning of its own. It is just *which roll of the dice you got.*

## What a seed can do

Because the seed fixes the starting noise, it gives you **reproducibility**, under one strict condition. If you keep the seed *and the prompt and every single setting* identical, you will get the identical image back, every time. Nothing changed, so nothing changes. That is genuinely useful: it lets you save a result, come back to it, and pick up exactly where you were.

So seeds are not useless. Hold that thought, we will come back to where they *do* earn their keep.

## What a seed cannot do

Here is where the dream dies. Lock your seed, generate "a woman with green eyes on a beach," and you get a face you like. Now keep that same seed and change the prompt to "a woman with green eyes in an office." Same dice, right? Should be the same woman?

It almost never is.

You will usually get a *different person*, sometimes subtly, sometimes completely. Because the seed only fixed the *starting noise*. It never fixed the *identity*. The moment you change the prompt, you have changed the instructions steering how that noise gets carved into an image, and the face that emerges shifts with it. The dice were the same, the game was different, the outcome was different.

Think back to the dice idea: **the seed is which roll you got, the prompt is the rules of the game.** Keep the dice, change the rules, and you get a completely different result. The seed was never holding your character. It was just holding the noise.

## The takeaway

There is no magic seed. You will never find one number that carries a character across different scenes, poses, and backgrounds, because that is simply not the job a seed does. A fixed seed reproduces an *entire identical image*. It does not reproduce a *person* across changing images.

**Use seeds for what they are good at:** reproducing an exact result, and doing controlled experiments. Change one thing, keep the seed fixed, and see what that one change actually did. That is a real workflow tool, and you will use it constantly.

**Do not ask seeds to do what they cannot:** hold an identity steady while everything around it changes. That requires feeding the character's *visual information* in directly, which, now that we have ruled out the easy fix, is exactly what the next lesson is about.
